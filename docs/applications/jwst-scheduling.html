James Webb Space Telescope Scheduling

Multi-Objective Scheduling · Momentum-Constrained

The Space Telescope Science Institute (STScI) Visit Scheduling System must arrange thousands of astronomical observations across a 365-day cycle, balancing scientific priority against the telescope's angular momentum limits, instrument configuration changes, and narrow visibility windows imposed by JWST's orbit around the Sun–Earth Lagrange point L2.

Scientific Context

The orbital mechanics and engineering constraints that shape the scheduling problem

Orbital & Mechanical Context

JWST orbits the Sun–Earth Lagrange point L2, roughly 1.5 million kilometres from Earth. Its five-layer sunshield blocks solar radiation but acts as a solar sail: photon pressure exerts torque on the spacecraft, gradually building angular momentum in the three reaction wheels.

Every observation at a given roll angle produces a deterministic momentum increment Δp(obs, roll). Over each 22-day momentum management cycle, the accumulated 3D momentum vector must remain within a polytope bounded by approximately 24–30 Nms. If the limit is breached, a costly thruster-based momentum dump is required, consuming finite fuel and interrupting science operations.

The roll angle is a continuous decision variable: each observation can be executed at any angle within a narrow band ±Δ around the nominal pointing, and the chosen angle determines both the momentum contribution and whether a suitable guide star is available for fine pointing control.

The Problem

Scheduling observations under visibility, momentum, and multi-objective constraints

Each astronomical observation is modelled as a job with a visibility window (the days during the year when the target is accessible from L2), a duration (exposure time in hours), an instrument configuration (NIRCam, NIRSpec, MIRI, FGS), and a scientific priority (1–10, assigned by the Time Allocation Committee).

The key constraint is momentum management: the 3D angular momentum vector must remain within an allowable polytope across every 22-day bin. The scheduler selects each observation's roll angle as a decision variable, which affects both the momentum delta and guide star availability. The problem becomes multi-objective: maximise total science time, minimise missed visibility windows, and minimise peak momentum accumulation.

Computational Complexity

  • The problem is NP-hard: it generalises job-shop scheduling with cumulative resource constraints.
  • Typical Cycle 1–3 instances contain 1,000–3,000 observations with overlapping windows.
  • The continuous roll-angle variable couples the combinatorial scheduling with nonlinear momentum dynamics.
  • STScI uses a multi-pass heuristic pipeline: long-range planning, short-term scheduling, then real-time patching.
  • Exact solvers (MIP) handle sub-problems of ~50 observations; the full problem requires decomposition or metaheuristics.

Solution Approaches

Three heuristics for momentum-constrained observation scheduling

Greedy Priority

Constructive Heuristic

Sort observations by scientific priority (descending). For each observation, schedule it at the earliest feasible day within its visibility window, ignoring momentum balance.

  • Sort observations by priority (highest first)
  • For each observation, find earliest day in window
  • Place observation; skip if no day available
  • No momentum checking — fast but may violate limits

Momentum-Balanced

Constraint-Aware Greedy

Sort by window urgency (latest deadline first). For each candidate day, evaluate the momentum contribution and choose the day that minimises momentum in the current 22-day bin while remaining feasible.

  • Sort observations by window deadline (earliest deadline first)
  • For each, iterate days in window
  • Select the day that minimises bin momentum
  • Skip observation if all days would exceed H_max

Pareto Greedy

Multi-Objective Heuristic

Alternates between science-optimal (highest priority first) and momentum-optimal (lowest delta first) insertion rounds, building a schedule that approximates the Pareto front.

  • Round A: pick highest-priority unscheduled observation
  • Schedule at day minimising momentum, respecting H_max
  • Round B: pick observation with smallest momentum delta
  • Alternate rounds until all observations placed or infeasible
