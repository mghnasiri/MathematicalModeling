Space Debris Collision Avoidance

Stochastic Optimisation · POMDP · Sequential Decision-Making

With over 35,000 tracked objects in orbit, satellite operators receive Conjunction Data Messages (CDMs) warning of potential collisions. Each CDM updates the probability of collision, but the operator must decide — manoeuvre now, wait for better data, or accept the risk — under deep uncertainty, with propellant budgets and mission continuity at stake.

Operational Context

How conjunction warnings drive collision avoidance decisions in LEO

Conjunction Data Messages

The 18th Space Defense Squadron (formerly 18th Space Control Squadron) at Vandenberg SFB issues Conjunction Data Messages (CDMs) approximately every 8 hours during a conjunction event. Each CDM provides the predicted Time of Closest Approach (TCA), the miss distance in the B-plane, and the computed Probability of Collision (PoC).

Critically, PoC evolves with each CDM update as tracking data improves: early estimates carry large covariance (uncertainty), meaning PoC can swing by orders of magnitude between updates. The operator faces a sequential decision under partial observability: manoeuvre now (consuming propellant and interrupting service), wait for the next CDM (risking insufficient time to manoeuvre), or accept the residual risk.

The Problem

Modelling collision avoidance as a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process

State space STrue orbital states + covariance matrices of satellite and debris
Observation space OCDM updates: PoC, miss distance, hours to TCA

Reward R(s,a)−cost(Δv) − service_loss + collision_avoided × asset_value
Discount γCDM inter-arrival interval (~6–8 hours)
POMDP Formulation Maximise   E[Σt γt R(st, at, st+1)]

// POMDP tuple: (S, A, T, R, O, Z, γ)
  S: continuous orbital state space
  A: {wait} ∪ {CAM(Δv, direction)}
  T(s'|s,a): orbital propagation dynamics // SGP4/numerical
  R(s,a): −cost(Δv) − service_loss + P(collision|s,a) × collision_penalty
  O: CDM observation (PoC, miss_distance, TCA_hours)
  Z(o|s'): measurement model for CDM generation
  γ: discount factor // CDMs arrive every 6–8 hours

Unlike classical deterministic scheduling problems, collision avoidance is fundamentally a sequential decision problem under partial observability. The true orbital state is never known exactly — only observed through noisy CDM measurements. The optimal policy must balance the information value of waiting (PoC may decrease with better data) against the risk of delayed action (less time for manoeuvre execution, higher Δv required).
