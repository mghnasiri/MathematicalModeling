Farm-to-Market Delivery

Capacitated Vehicle Routing

A cooperative of farms near Quebec City distributes fresh produce to 15 delivery points — farmers markets, restaurant kitchens, grocery stores, and food banks. A fleet of refrigerated trucks departs daily from the cooperative's distribution center. This is the Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem — one of the most studied and practically important problems in Operations Research, applied here to the farm-to-market fresh food supply chain.

The Problem

Why route optimization matters for fresh produce distribution

Consider a farm cooperative's distribution center located in Quebec City. Every morning, a fleet of refrigerated trucks departs loaded with fresh produce — vegetables, dairy, meats, and artisanal products — bound for delivery points across the region. These include bustling farmers markets like Marché du Vieux-Port and Marché Jean-Talon QC, acclaimed restaurants such as Panache and Légende, major grocery chains, and community food banks like Moisson Québec.

Each truck has a maximum capacity of 2,000 kg, and each delivery point requires a specific quantity of produce. Every truck must start and end at the cooperative's depot, and every delivery point must be served exactly once. The objective is to minimize total travel distance across all routes, which directly reduces fuel costs, driver hours, and — critically for perishable goods — time that produce spends in transit.

This problem is NP-hard: with 15 delivery stops, the number of possible route configurations grows super-exponentially. Even modest improvements of 5–10% in total route distance translate to significant annual savings for a cooperative managing daily deliveries. The CVRP was introduced by Dantzig & Ramser (1959) as a generalization of the Traveling Salesman Problem and remains one of the most active areas of research in combinatorial optimization.

The Model

Mathematical formulation of the CVRP

CVRP Formulation minimize   Σk Σi Σj cij · xijk   // total travel distance
subject to
  Σi∈Sk di · yik ≤ Q   ∀k   // vehicle capacity
  Σk yik = 1   ∀i   // each customer visited once
  // subtour elimination constraints
  xijk ∈ {0, 1}   // binary assignment of edge (i,j) to vehicle k

Where cij is the Haversine distance (km) between stops i and j, di is the demand (kg) at stop i, and Q is the truck capacity (2,000 kg).

The Algorithms

Three classical approaches to vehicle routing

Clarke-Wright Savings

Heuristic O(n² log n) 1964

The savings-based merge heuristic starts by assigning each customer to its own dedicated route (depot → customer → depot). It then computes a savings value for every pair of customers: s(i,j) = d(0,i) + d(0,j) − d(i,j). Pairs are sorted by descending savings, and routes are greedily merged when the combined load does not exceed vehicle capacity. This approach captures the key insight that visiting nearby customers together saves two depot trips.

function clarke_wright(customers, Q): // savings s(i,j) = d(0,i)+d(0,j)-d(i,j) savings = compute all s(i,j) sort savings descending routes = {[i] for each customer i} for each (i,j) in savings: if can_merge(route_of(i), route_of(j), Q): merge routes return routes

Sweep Algorithm

Heuristic O(n log n) 1974

The sweep algorithm exploits the geographic distribution of customers. It computes the polar angle from the depot to each customer, sorts customers by angle, and sweeps a ray around the depot. Customers are added to the current route until the vehicle capacity is reached, then a new route begins. This spatial decomposition is extremely fast and works well when customers are geographically clustered.

function sweep(customers, Q): // Sort by angle from depot sort customers by atan2(y-y0, x-x0) route = [], load = 0, routes = [] for each customer c: if load + c.demand > Q: routes.add(route) route = []; load = 0 route.add(c); load += c.demand routes.add(route) return routes

Nearest Neighbor

Heuristic O(n²) 1956

Adapted from TSP, the nearest neighbor heuristic builds routes greedily. Starting from the depot, it visits the closest unvisited customer whose demand fits within the remaining truck capacity. When no feasible customer remains, the truck returns to the depot and a new route begins. Simple and fast, but myopic decisions can lead to suboptimal final routes.

function nearest_neighbor(customers, Q): while unvisited customers remain: cur = depot, route = [], load = 0 while true: next = nearest unvisited with demand + load ≤ Q if none: break route.add(next); load += next.demand cur = next routes.add(route) return routes

Why It's More Complex

What this demo does not capture

Production-Grade Challenges

  • Time windows for market opening hours and restaurant prep times — farmers markets open at specific hours, restaurants need deliveries before lunch prep begins
  • Perishable goods requiring temperature-controlled routing — cold chain logistics demand minimizing time in transit, with different temperature zones per product category
  • Dynamic demand updates from mobile ordering — restaurants and markets may adjust orders in real-time based on previous day's sales
  • Multi-depot operations with multiple farm pickup points — produce may originate from different farms, requiring coordinated pickup and consolidation
  • Driver work-time regulations and break requirements — Quebec labor standards require rest periods, limiting consecutive driving hours
  • Seasonal demand surges during harvest festivals — events like the Quebec City Agricultural Fair can triple normal demand volumes
  • Vehicle heterogeneity — different truck sizes with different refrigeration capabilities for dairy, produce, and meat products
